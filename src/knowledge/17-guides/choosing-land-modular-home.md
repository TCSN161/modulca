# How to Choose Land for a Modular Home

Choosing the right plot of land is the single most important decision in your modular home project. A bad site can add tens of thousands of euros in unexpected costs or make modular delivery impossible. This guide covers everything you need to evaluate before buying.

## The Modular Difference: Why Land Selection Matters More

Traditional construction adapts to site conditions with on-site labor. Modular construction delivers pre-built modules by truck and assembles them with a crane. This means your site must meet specific access and logistics requirements that traditional builds can ignore.

Key constraints unique to modular:

- **Road access for long trucks** (typically 12–16 m trailers)
- **Crane positioning area** near the foundation
- **Turning radius** for delivery vehicles
- **No overhead obstructions** (power lines, trees) along the delivery route

If your plot fails any of these, modular construction becomes significantly more expensive or impossible.

## Step 1: Check Legal and Zoning Status

Before falling in love with a view, verify the legal fundamentals.

### Ownership and Title

- [ ] Obtain an **extras de Carte Funciara** (land registry extract) — not older than 30 days
- [ ] Verify the seller is the actual owner (check for multiple owners, inheritance disputes)
- [ ] Check for **sarcini** (encumbrances): mortgages, liens, easements, or usage rights
- [ ] Confirm the land has a **numar cadastral** (cadastral number) and is properly registered
- [ ] Verify there are no pending legal disputes (litigii) on the property

### Zoning: PUG and PUZ

- [ ] Request a **Certificat de Urbanism** (CU) before purchasing — this is the most critical step
- [ ] Check the **PUG** (Plan Urbanistic General) for the area — is the land zoned residential?
- [ ] Check if a **PUZ** (Plan Urbanistic Zonal) exists for the area with more specific regulations
- [ ] Verify the **POT** (ground coverage) — typically 35–40% for residential
- [ ] Verify the **CUT** (floor area ratio) — determines total buildable area
- [ ] Check height regime — how many stories are permitted
- [ ] Check minimum and maximum setbacks from boundaries and road

### Common Zoning Categories

| Zone Code | Meaning | Buildable? |
|-----------|---------|-----------|
| LIR / Lis | Residential (low density) | Yes |
| LIR-a | Residential (individual houses) | Yes |
| LIM | Mixed residential | Yes |
| A | Agricultural | No (requires zoning change) |
| P | Protected area | Restricted |
| V | Green space | No |

**Warning:** Agricultural land (extravilan) requires an expensive and slow process to convert to intravilan (buildable) status. Budget 6–18 months and 2,000–10,000 EUR for this conversion, and there is no guarantee of approval.

## Step 2: Evaluate Access and Delivery Logistics

This is where modular site selection differs most from traditional construction.

### Road Access Requirements

- [ ] Access road width: minimum **4 m** for delivery trucks, ideally **6 m+**
- [ ] Road surface: must support **30–40 tonnes** (loaded modular truck)
- [ ] No low bridges or tunnels on the delivery route (minimum clearance **4.5 m**)
- [ ] No sharp turns with radius under **12 m** on the final approach
- [ ] No weight-restricted bridges between the factory and your site
- [ ] Verify the access road is a public road or you have a legal right of passage (servitute de trecere)

### Crane Access

- [ ] Flat area adjacent to the foundation: minimum **6 x 12 m** for a mobile crane
- [ ] Ground must support the crane's weight (typically 40–60 tonnes with outriggers)
- [ ] No overhead power lines within the crane's swing radius
- [ ] Clear vertical space above the foundation for module placement

### Delivery Route Survey

Before committing to a plot, trace the delivery route from the nearest modular factory:

- [ ] Identify the factory location and calculate the route
- [ ] Check for road restrictions (weight limits, height limits, width limits)
- [ ] Note any seasonal restrictions (some rural roads are impassable in spring due to mud)
- [ ] Calculate transport cost: typically **500–2,000 EUR** depending on distance

### Tips

- Use Google Street View to pre-check road conditions and turning points.
- Ask the modular manufacturer to do a delivery feasibility assessment. Most will do this for free.

## Step 3: Assess Utilities and Infrastructure

### Essential Utilities Checklist

- [ ] **Electricity**: Is there a power line within 200 m? Connection cost: 500–3,000 EUR
- [ ] **Water**: Municipal water supply available? If not, well drilling costs 1,500–5,000 EUR
- [ ] **Sewage**: Municipal sewer connection available? If not, budget for a septic system (1,500–4,000 EUR) or bio-station (3,000–8,000 EUR)
- [ ] **Natural gas**: Available nearby? Connection cost: 1,000–4,000 EUR. Alternative: heat pump (no gas needed)
- [ ] **Internet**: Fiber optic, cable, or only mobile? Check with providers for the specific address

### Utility Connection Timeline

| Utility | Typical Connection Time | Cost Range (EUR) |
|---------|------------------------|------------------|
| Electricity (existing line nearby) | 2–6 months | 500–3,000 |
| Electricity (new transformer needed) | 6–12 months | 3,000–15,000 |
| Water (municipal) | 1–3 months | 500–2,000 |
| Water (well) | 1–2 weeks drilling | 1,500–5,000 |
| Sewage (municipal) | 1–3 months | 500–2,000 |
| Sewage (septic/bio) | 1–2 weeks installation | 1,500–8,000 |
| Gas | 3–6 months | 1,000–4,000 |

### Off-Grid Considerations

If utilities are far away, consider whether an off-grid or semi-off-grid approach makes sense:

- Solar panels + battery storage for electricity
- Well + water treatment for water
- Bio-station for sewage
- Heat pump for heating (no gas needed)

This can actually be cheaper than long-distance utility connections in rural areas.

## Step 4: Evaluate Topography and Soil

### Topography

- [ ] **Slope**: Flat to gentle slope (under 10%) is ideal for modular. Steeper slopes require expensive retaining walls and complex foundations.
- [ ] **Orientation**: South-facing slopes are best for solar gain and energy efficiency
- [ ] **Drainage**: Does water pool on the site after rain? Low-lying plots may have flooding risk
- [ ] **Elevation**: Check if the plot is in a flood risk zone (check with Apele Romane / local authorities)

### Soil Quality

- [ ] Commission a **studiu geotehnic** (geotechnical study) — costs 300–800 EUR
- [ ] Check soil bearing capacity — modular homes need consistent load-bearing ground
- [ ] Identify groundwater level — high water table complicates foundations
- [ ] Check for expansive clay (argila) — common in many Romanian regions, causes foundation movement

### Foundation Types by Soil Condition

| Soil Condition | Recommended Foundation | Cost Impact |
|---------------|----------------------|-------------|
| Good bearing, low water table | Strip foundation or slab | Baseline |
| High water table | Elevated slab or pile foundation | +30–50% |
| Expansive clay | Deep strip or pile foundation | +40–60% |
| Rocky ground | Adapted strip foundation | +20–30% |
| Steep slope | Retaining walls + adapted foundation | +50–100% |

### Romanian Climate Zones

Consider the climate zone when evaluating a plot:

- **Zone I (coast/south)**: Mild winters, lower heating costs, watch for wind exposure
- **Zone II (plains)**: Hot summers, moderate winters, generally good for modular
- **Zone III (hills/transition)**: More temperature variation, good balance
- **Zone IV (mountains)**: High snow loads, cold winters — requires robust insulation and stronger structure

## Step 5: Red Flags to Watch For

Stop and reconsider if you encounter any of these:

### Legal Red Flags

- Land has no cadastral number or Carte Funciara entry
- Seller cannot provide a clear ownership chain
- Land is classified as agricultural (extravilan) with no PUZ in progress
- Protected historical or natural zone restrictions
- Pending litigation on the property
- Multiple co-owners who have not all agreed to sell

### Physical Red Flags

- No paved road access (dirt roads may be impassable for delivery trucks)
- Overhead high-voltage power lines crossing the plot
- Visible signs of flooding (water marks on neighboring buildings, standing water)
- Adjacent industrial or waste facilities
- Very steep terrain (over 15% slope)
- Neighboring construction that blocks access for crane operations

### Financial Red Flags

- Price significantly below market rate (investigate why)
- Utility connections would cost more than 10,000 EUR total
- Zoning change required with uncertain outcome
- Access road improvements needed at your expense

## Site Selection Checklist Summary

Use this consolidated checklist when evaluating any plot:

### Must-Have (Deal Breakers)

- [ ] Legal ownership is clear and undisputed
- [ ] Zoned residential (intravilan) or zoning change is approved
- [ ] Road access adequate for modular delivery trucks
- [ ] Crane can be positioned near the build area
- [ ] Electricity available within reasonable distance
- [ ] Water source available (municipal or well)
- [ ] No flood zone or protected area restrictions
- [ ] Geotechnical conditions are suitable for standard foundations

### Nice-to-Have

- [ ] South-facing orientation
- [ ] Municipal sewage connection available
- [ ] Natural gas available
- [ ] Fiber internet available
- [ ] Flat or gentle slope (under 5%)
- [ ] Neighbors have already built (indicates proven buildability)
- [ ] Paved access road

### Investigate Further

- [ ] Delivery route fully surveyed
- [ ] All utility connection costs estimated
- [ ] Foundation cost estimated based on geotechnical study
- [ ] CU obtained and reviewed with an architect
