# Renaissance Architecture (1400 – 1600)

## Overview
The Renaissance ("rebirth") marked a return to classical Greek and Roman principles — proportion, symmetry, geometry, and human-centered design. Beginning in Florence and spreading across Europe, Renaissance architecture replaced Gothic complexity with rational clarity.

## Key Figures

| Architect | Period | Contribution | Key Work |
|-----------|--------|-------------|----------|
| Brunelleschi | 1377-1446 | Perspective, dome engineering | Florence Cathedral dome |
| Alberti | 1404-1472 | Architectural theory, facade design | "De Re Aedificatoria" (1452) |
| Bramante | 1444-1514 | Central-plan churches | Tempietto, Rome |
| Palladio | 1508-1580 | Villa typology, Four Books | Villa Rotonda, Vicenza |
| Michelangelo | 1475-1564 | Sculptural architecture | St. Peter's dome, Laurentian Library |

## Renaissance Principles

| Principle | Description | Legacy |
|-----------|-------------|--------|
| Symmetry | Bilateral balance around central axis | Still fundamental in residential design |
| Proportion | Mathematical ratios (1:1, 1:2, 2:3) | Room proportions in Neufert |
| Classical orders | Doric, Ionic, Corinthian revived | Column and pilaster language |
| Central plan | Geometric purity (circle, square) | Centralized spaces |
| Human scale | Building relates to human body | Ergonomic design |
| Rational structure | Visible logic in construction | Honest structure |

## Palladio's Influence

Andrea Palladio's "I Quattro Libri dell'Architettura" (1570) codified residential architecture:
- **Villa typology**: Central hall + symmetrical wings — still the template for country houses
- **Room proportions**: Rooms in ratios of 1:1, 1:√2, 1:2, 2:3
- **Indoor-outdoor**: Loggias and porticoes connecting house to landscape
- **Modular grid**: Villa plans based on repeating room modules
