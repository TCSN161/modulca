# Proportion, Scale & Human Measure

## Overview
Good architecture relates to the human body. Proportion creates visual harmony; scale ensures buildings feel right to inhabit. From Vitruvius through Le Corbusier to modern ergonomics, the human body is the measure of all architecture.

## Key Concepts

### Proportion
The mathematical relationship between parts of a building and the whole:
- **Symmetry**: Visual balance (not necessarily mirror-image)
- **Rhythm**: Repetition of elements (columns, windows, modules)
- **Hierarchy**: Most important spaces are largest or tallest
- **Unity**: All parts relate to a coherent whole

### Scale
How a building's size relates to the human body:
- **Human scale**: Elements sized for human comfort (doors, stairs, counters)
- **Monumental scale**: Intentionally large to impress (cathedrals, civic buildings)
- **Intimate scale**: Deliberately small and cozy (alcoves, window seats)

## Le Corbusier's Modulor
A harmonic measurement system based on:
- Human height: 1.83m (6 feet)
- Navel height: 1.13m (divides body at golden section)
- Two scales: Red series (body dimensions) and Blue series (raised arm)
- All dimensions derive from Fibonacci sequence ratios
- Applied to his Unité d'Habitation and Chandigarh buildings

## ModulCA Application
The 3×3m module is a human-scale unit:
- 3.00m width = comfortable room span (1.5× arm span)
- 2.70m ceiling = 1.5× human height (spacious but not vast)
- 7m² usable area = adequate for one person's primary room function
- Module combinations scale predictably: 2 modules = couple, 3+ = family
